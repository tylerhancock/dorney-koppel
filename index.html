Pennies per Person?
"People Need to Wake Up."

30 million Americans have COPD and 12-15 million don't even know it. Just last year alone, 155,000 people died from COPD.

30 Million are Suffering

Our country allocates mere pennies per person on COPD research each year. Pennies, to fight a disease that kills more Americans every year than in the last 4 wars—Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan, and Iraq—combined. That's the message we're sending to people struggling with COPD.

Most people know Chronic Pulmonary Obstructive Disease (COPD) by other names—like emphysema or chronic bronchitis. It's a chronic lung condition that gets worse with time, and makes those that suffer from it feel like they can never catch their breath.

Why is the #3 Killer 165th in Funding?

Right now, someone you know has COPD. So why is the #3 killer among chronic diseases just 165th in congressional funding? We can't explain it, but we can change it. With a greater focus on treatments, respiratory therapies, education and, ultimately, a cure, we can create hope for people living with this disease. That's why the MoreForCOPD initiative exists. We're petitioning Congress and demanding appropriate funding for COPD research today.

Our Story

Beau, 9/11 First Responder

Beau, 9/11 First Responder

"Neglected, Not Taken Care of By My Own Government."

We all know someone with COPD. They're our mothers, fathers, friends, and neighbors. Our truck drivers, teachers, athletes, and artists. They're from small towns in West Virginia and big cities like New York. They're people, Americans, just like us.

Some got it from smoking. Others from where they worked or lived. And some never touched a cigarette a day in their life. But they all share one thing: they're fighting this devastating disease while fighting for air.

Their lives are worth more than pennies.
These are their stories.

We Are The COPD Americans

Beau

Beau is a former carpenter who used his skills at Ground Zero on 9/11 to help keep other responders safe from surrounding debris. Never a smoker, he contracted COPD from the fumes he was exposed to there and throughout his time performing bridge-repair steelwork. Beau got COPD from helping others, so where's the help he deserves?

On the need for change:

“People need to wake up and hear these things. I hope the politicians start listening. If somebody would [just] do something. They just have to try. It's like I always tell my daughter: ‘All you can do is try. If you don't try, you didn't do anything.’”

Imtiaz

Imtiaz was born in Karachi, Pakistan. When he gained his American citizenship, he worked as an engineer at the World Trade Center. After the attacks, Imtiaz returned to work in the polluted air and picked up a smoking habit—two factors that likely contributed to his COPD.

On his experience at Ground Zero:

“The whole world was upside down-- being of my [Muslim] faith and being from the other part of the world. After 9/11, I worked several years in the same vicinity; the air wasn't healthy enough for the people that were living or working there. And I was there every day.”

On why we need more for COPD:

“This is the world’s richest, and a big, country. It should spend more money in research on this type of disease if there are…people dying or suffering.”

Gail

Gail works in the department of medicine at an outpatient hospital. A former dancer, she’s been active throughout her life. When she was diagnosed with COPD, she quit smoking, and knew that staying active would be a struggle that mattered to her.

On physical activity with COPD:

“I taught ballet for 27 years before I went to work for the hospital. I've always been very active. I used to run. Now I walk. I can walk at a pretty quick pace. But if I try to run, my breathing is affected.”

On the injustice of current COPD funding:

“Perhaps we need a big change in Congress.”

Martin

As an EMT, paramedic, and Port Authority police officer, Martin has dedicated himself to serving New York City. Despite not being officially summoned to the scene, he spent six months working tirelessly at Ground Zero. Martin's life, along with the other COPD Americans, is worth so much more.

On his experience at Ground Zero:

“I can close my eyes now. And I can see a woman's shoes, I could see blueprints, handbags…I’ll never forget. It was like snow on the ground. It was this powdery, gray substance…that was concrete. That was desks. That was computers. That was people. That was everything made and mushed when that building collapsed and it pancaked.”

On making noise to make change:

“Squeaky wheel gets the oil. If people aren't out promoting it, explaining what's going on, people aren't aware. You need to start getting funding for it. We need to start trying to find out what we can do to either prevent and treat and extend people's lives from this.”

Rhonda

Rhonda spent 14 years in the police force—starting in traffic patrol and working her way up. On duty when disaster struck the World Trade Center, she spent the next few months on 12-hour shifts at the site. She ended up with a COPD diagnosis that she says cut her life in half. That's why pennies just won't cut it.

On her experience at Ground Zero:

“My breathing was labored. I put a bandana around my face. There was a lot of fire around. Really, it was like hell. Everything was burning.”

On fighting for her breath:

“[One day], I was driving to work and I couldn't breathe. And I called the job and said, ‘I can't come in.’ And they said, ‘you're mandated.’ And I said, ‘I can't breathe.”

Jim

Jim is a recently retired artist and musician. He’s never touched a cigarette—attributing his COPD to the asbestos-containing adhesives and glues that were essential for his work, as well as his parents’ heavy smoking throughout his childhood. The physical limitations of COPD have robbed Jim of one of his favorite hobbies—playing drums.

On life with COPD:

“[I] get up from bed in the morning to go to the bathroom, which is 20 feet away, and I [have] to race back to get to my nebulizer…because it was just unbelievably hard to breathe.”

Sandy & Terry

Terry and Sandy are both longtime smokers; Terry was the first to be diagnosed with COPD. When she found pulmonary rehabilitation, she was able to get her life and health back on track.

On pulmonary rehabilitation:

“When I got outta there I was like a new person. I don't have to use my oxygen. I can walk, you know, where I couldn't even do that kinda stuff. Go up and down stairs and all that...”

Later, Sandy also received a diagnosis—and turned to pulmonary rehabilitation. This decision proved a crucial one when a future cancer diagnosis would require surgery on her lungs. She credits the breathing, walking, and movement techniques taught in pulmonary therapy for her smooth post-surgical recovery.

On getting the word out about COPD:

“You'd be surprised at the people you just talk to on a daily basis that know nothin' about this stuff…there's so many people on a daily basis that have no clue."

Larry

Larry spent 35 years working in the coal mines of West Virginia. Growing up, he witnessed the damage mine work did to the lungs of his father and his brothers, but believed that improvements to the mines would be the difference for him. It wasn’t. Today, he combats his condition with an oxygen machine and a regimen of pulmonary rehabilitation.

On living with COPD:

“You can't do what you'd really want to or what you used to do. I used to do a lot of hunting and I don't even try that any more…I can get a job done but just ten times longer than it used to.”
